## Soloing Over Changes: A Harmonic Framework for Guitar

(Written by Claude Sonnet 4.6)

### The Central Principle: Tonal Gravity

Every chord progression exists within a harmonic context — a tonal center, a mode, or a deliberate ambiguity between them. Your job as a soloist is to understand *why* a chord is in a progression, not just *what* it is. That "why" tells you which notes will sound resolved, which will create tension, and which will sound wrong. The key you solo in at any given moment flows from that understanding.

---

### The Major Key Foundation

In a major-key progression, the seven chords built on the scale degrees (I, IIm, IIIm, IV, V, Vmaj7, VIm, VIIdim) are all diatonic — they all belong to the same parent scale. Over any of these chords, you can play the parent major scale and every note will be *available*, though not equally stable. This is the "one key, all chords" approach.

**Example:** In G major (G, Am, Bm, C, D, Em, F#dim), you can solo in G major over the entire progression. The scale works because every chord is a subset of the same seven notes.

However, "available" is not the same as "idiomatic." Over the IIm and VIm chords, the same notes now imply a Dorian or Aeolian color respectively, even though the pitch content is identical. Experienced players *lean into* those modal colors by emphasizing chord tones and characteristic notes of whichever mode that chord implies within the key.

---

### Minor Keys and Modal Ambiguity

Minor is more complex because there are three practical minor scales in common use:

- **Natural minor (Aeolian):** The default. Works over Im, IVm, Vm, bVI, bVII.
- **Harmonic minor:** Raises the 7th, creating a leading tone and a V7 chord. Use it specifically when the V chord is dominant (V7), which is common in jazz and classical-influenced writing. The raised 7th is a "visiting" note, not a constant.
- **Melodic minor (ascending):** Raises both the 6th and 7th. Used in jazz constantly (see below). Produces a smoother, less "dark" sound going upward.

**The practical rule:** When you see a minor progression with a major V or V7 (e.g., Am - Dm - E7), that E7 is pulling toward Am harmonically. Over the E7, shift to A harmonic minor (which contains E7's notes: E, G#, B, D). Over the rest, use A natural minor. The G# (major 7th) over the E7 is a *chromatic inflection* within an otherwise natural-minor solo.

---

### The Blues: Intentional Harmonic Friction

Blues deliberately violates diatonic rules, and this is where a lot of guitarists first encounter "outside" playing without realizing it.

A standard 12-bar blues in A uses **A7, D7, and E7** — all dominant seventh chords. In strict diatonic terms, you can't have three dominant sevenths in the same key. Yet the blues pentatonic minor scale (A, C, D, Eb, E, G) works over *all three chords*, including the b3 (C natural over an A7 that contains C#). This works because:

1. **Blues operates in a parallel modal space.** The b3, b5, and b7 are expressive devices, not errors. The "blue notes" create tension that *resolves into* the chord rather than conflicting with it in a way the ear rejects.
2. **The dominant 7th chord is itself already a tension chord.** A7 wants to resolve; the dissonance of the minor pentatonic *adds* to that tension expressively.
3. **Root-based thinking:** The pentatonic is built on the tonic, not the key signature. You're following the *root movement* of the changes more than the internal chord quality.

**The upgrade:** Over each individual chord in a blues, you can also use the *major pentatonic* of that chord's root — A major pent over A7, D major pent over D7, E major pent over E7. This "follows the changes" more precisely and gives a brighter, more resolved sound. Many great soloists blend minor and major pentatonic, landing on major 3rds on beat one of each chord change.

---

### Jazz: Following Every Chord

Jazz harmony demands the most rigorous approach. The standard method is to assign a specific scale to *each chord* individually, then navigate between them. The chord type determines the scale:

| Chord Type | Scale |
|---|---|
| Major 7 (Imaj7) | Ionian (major) or Lydian (raised 4th, softer) |
| Dominant 7 (V7) | Mixolydian, or altered scale over secondary dominants |
| Minor 7 (IIm7) | Dorian (most common), or Aeolian |
| Minor 7b5 (half-dim) | Locrian or Locrian #2 (raised 9th) |
| Diminished 7 | Diminished (whole-half) scale |
| Minor-major 7 | Melodic minor |
| Dominant 7#11 | Lydian dominant (melodic minor built a 5th below) |

The **II-V-I** is the atomic unit of jazz harmony. In C major: Dm7 - G7 - Cmaj7. Over Dm7 you play D Dorian; over G7 you play G Mixolydian; over Cmaj7 you play C Ionian. In practice all three scales share the same seven notes (C major), so smooth voice leading is built in. The real skill is *targeting* chord tones on the downbeats of each change.

**Secondary dominants** are where this gets interesting. If a progression has an A7 leading to Dm7 in an otherwise C major context, the A7 is a secondary dominant (V7/ii). Over that A7, you can play A Mixolydian (which contains C#, foreign to C major) or, more aggressively, the **A altered scale** (7th mode of Bb melodic minor), which contains both the b9 and #9, the b5 and #5 — maximum tension before resolution.

---

### Borrowed Chords and Modal Mixture

Many rock and pop progressions borrow chords from the parallel minor. A progression like I - bVII - bVI - V (e.g., A - G - F - E) is extremely common in rock. The bVII and bVI are borrowed from A natural minor, yet the V (E major) is from A major/harmonic minor.

The approach here: **follow the tonal center, not the mode**. A minor pentatonic (or A natural minor) works over the whole thing because the overall *feel* is minor/Aeolian, even though the V chord is major. Over the E major specifically, you can briefly emphasize the G# to honor the leading tone — just don't let it clash through the bVII and bVI where G natural is expected.

A purely major progression with a bVII chord (like E - D - A in classic rock) often supports **E Mixolydian** (same as A major, but centered on E). The b7 (D) is the characteristic note. E minor pentatonic also works because it shares five notes with E Mixolydian, and the overlap is in all the "safe" places.

---

### The Modal Approach: Choosing a Mode for a Static or Ambiguous Chord

When a progression *stays on one chord for a long time*, or when a chord's function is ambiguous, you choose the mode that best fits its internal quality and context:

- **Sus4 or Vsus chord, static:** Dorian or Mixolydian. Neither has a 3rd, so both fit.
- **Major chord with no clear key:** Try Lydian (raised 4th) for a floating, cinematic sound vs. Ionian for grounded.
- **Minor chord, long duration:** Dorian for a bright minor feel (natural 6th), Aeolian for darker/more melancholic, Phrygian for Spanish/metal tension (b2), Phrygian dominant (harmonic minor 5th mode) for flamenco or Middle Eastern.
- **Dominant 7, static (like a funk groove):** Mixolydian is the neutral choice. Lydian dominant adds a #11 for a more "outside" color. The blues scale adds grit.

---

### The Logic, Summarized

The decision tree works roughly like this:

1. **What is the tonal center and overall mode of the progression?** This gives you your default palette.
2. **Is each chord diatonic to that center?** If yes, the parent scale works throughout; vary your emphasis by chord.
3. **Are there chromatic chords — secondary dominants, borrowed chords, modal mixture?** For each one, identify *why it's there* (where it's going) and inflect your scale accordingly, especially on chord tones.
4. **What genre are you in?** Blues permits dissonance that jazz would handle more carefully. Rock often stays pentatonic and ignores inner chord quality for feel. Jazz treats every chord as a separate scale target.
5. **What is the *duration* of each chord?** Fast-moving changes favor the parent scale with targeted chord tones. Slow or static harmonies reward committing fully to the chord's own scale.

The underlying principle is always the same: **notes are not right or wrong in isolation — they are right or wrong in relation to the chord beneath them, the chord they're moving toward, and the overall tonal gravity of the progression.** Scale choice is the framework; the ear adjudicates everything.
